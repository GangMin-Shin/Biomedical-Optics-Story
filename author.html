About Gang Min Shin

Gang Min Shin completed his master's degree in the Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science at DGIST. His thesis was titled Pulsatile Waveform Analysis Using Diffuse Optical Flowmetry: A Study of the Prefrontal Cortex and Finger.

Research Field

His research focuses on biomedical optics, diffuse optical flowmetry, and noninvasive measurement of microcirculatory blood-flow dynamics. In particular, he studies physiological signals obtained using Multi-Mode Fiber Diffuse Speckle Contrast Analysis (MMF-DSCA).

Main Research Theme

The main goal of his research is to analyze pulsatile blood-flow waveforms rather than relying only on average blood-flow changes. For this purpose, Blood Flow Index (BFI), Blood Volume Index (BVI), heart-rate-related features, and frequency-domain components are analyzed together.

Prefrontal Cortex Study

One part of the research investigated hemodynamic responses in the right prefrontal cortex during cognitive-load tasks. Measurements were performed using source-detector separation channels, and the resulting BFI and BVI signals were analyzed in both the time domain and the frequency domain.

The study examined how cognitive workload affects optical blood-flow signals, including task-related waveform changes and spectral components such as cardiac, low-frequency oscillation, and very-low-frequency oscillation bands.

Finger Microcirculation Study

Another part of the research analyzed peripheral microcirculatory responses in the finger during ST36 stimulation and the Cold Pressor Test (CPT). These experiments were designed to examine autonomic and vascular responses through BFI, BVI, heart rate, heart rate variability, and inter-beat interval analysis.

This work emphasized that physiological interpretation should consider blood flow, blood volume, pulsatile waveform morphology, and frequency-domain features together.
