Diffuse Speckle Contrast Analysis (DSCA)

Diffuse Speckle Contrast Analysis (DSCA) is a camera-based diffuse optical flowmetry method. It estimates blood-flow-related dynamics from speckle blurring caused by moving scatterers, mainly red blood cells, and estimates blood-volume-related information from detected intensity changes.

1. Real DSCA System

The actual DSCA instrument integrates the laser source, source fiber outputs, detector fiber inputs, and camera-based speckle acquisition path in one optical measurement system.

  • The real system provides source channels for tissue illumination and detector channels for collecting diffusely scattered light.
  • The detected speckle images are processed frame-by-frame to extract BFI and BVI.
  • The hardware layout is designed for simultaneous multi-channel diffuse optical flowmetry.

1. Speckle Contrast

DSCA measures speckle blurring during finite camera exposure. Faster red-blood-cell motion causes stronger decorrelation, stronger speckle blurring, and lower speckle contrast.

K = σI / <I>
K² = σI² / <I>²

2. Blood Flow Index

BFI is derived from speckle contrast. In relative-flow interpretation, increased red-blood-cell motion lowers K² and increases the flow-related index.

BFIrel ∝ 1 / K²

3. Blood Volume Index

BVI is derived from detected intensity. When blood volume increases, optical absorption increases, detected intensity decreases, and BVI increases.

BVI = −log(Σ Ipixels)
